Abstract

Automated Classroom Dialogue Encoding (ACDE) is critical for understanding instructional quality, yet evaluations of large language models (LLMs) often reduce classroom talk to surface classification. We propose DialogEval, a benchmark grounded in a Three Bs framework that probes discourse reasoning Between, Behind, and Beyond the words. We evaluate representative LLMs across FIAC, IRF, and SEDA. Results reveal a Logic Threshold for contextual dependency, show that logic-driven reasoning scaffolds can outperform rule-heavy manuals, and diagnose failures such as Domain Rigidity, Expertise Reversal, and Conceptual Granularity Collapse.

The Three Bs Framework

DialogEval operationalizes classroom discourse decoding into three cognitive dimensions, forming an evolutionary gradient of inference demand.

1. Between the Words

Contextual Logic (IRF)

Resolving local discourse dependencies and functional sequences, rather than anchoring on isolated cues.

2. Behind the Words

Pedagogical Intent (FIAC)

Inferring latent instructional intent and how prompting density reshapes intent decoding.

3. Beyond the Words

Sociocultural Norms (SEDA)

Interpreting implicit norms and how domain/geocultural priors can help—or interfere.

Figure 1: The Three Bs framework for Automated Classroom Dialogue Encoding (ACDE). The benchmark probes increasing cognitive demands: contextual logic (Between), latent pedagogical intent (Behind), and sociocultural norm inference (Beyond).

Benchmark Construction

DialogEval is built from authentic classroom discourse and annotated under three canonical frameworks (FIAC, IRF, SEDA), enabling cross-tradition evaluation from structural coding to sociocultural inference.

  • Multi-framework labels: FIAC (intent), IRF (local logic), SEDA (norms).
  • Expert ground truth: dual annotation with adjudication for disagreement.
  • Two subject domains: Arts and Science to test schema effects.

Availability: Evaluation scripts, prompt templates, and annotation protocols will be released after the anonymous review period. Raw classroom transcripts will be shared in a controlled and anonymized form due to ethical constraints.

Methodology

Sliding Window Context Modeling

Each target utterance is evaluated within a fixed neighborhood window (two preceding and two following turns), while the model labels only the center turn.

Figure 3: Sliding-window evaluation protocol. Bounded local context reduces ambiguity while mitigating long-context noise.

Prompting Hierarchy (P1–P4)

DialogEval varies instructional density through four prompt levels, enabling controlled tests of whether reasoning processes outperform rule accumulation.

P1 Vanilla (zero-shot): label options only.
P2 Definition (zero-shot): add category definitions.
P3 Expert manual (few-shot): add scenario-based examples.
P4 CoT scaffold: add an explicit reasoning path for labeling.
Example task: IRF category Initiation (teacher starts a new exchange). We illustrate how increasing prompt granularity changes the model’s decision path.

Key Findings

Logic Threshold

Contextual dependency in IRF exhibits a threshold effect: models often require explicit reasoning scaffolds to disambiguate feedback vs. mixed functions, and scaling alone is insufficient.

Process Over Rules

Rule-heavy manuals can overload models, while logic-driven CoT prompting strengthens reasoning pathways in high-inference settings.

Domain Rigidity & Expertise Reversal

Domain-specific models may underperform on novel frameworks, showing schema interference where prior pedagogical templates override task instructions.

Conceptual Granularity Collapse

In SEDA, models often collapse fine-grained discourse moves into broad categories, revealing limits in sociocultural norm decoding.

Capillary Diagnostics: Lure Words & Cognitive Bias

DialogEval introduces Capillary Analysis to trace misclassifications to high-risk lexical triggers. We quantify a word’s relative error risk (RER) and categorize failures into recurring pathways.

Hallucinated Interactivity

Fillers and deixis can trigger false agency detection (e.g., silence interpreted as initiation).

Semantic Anchoring

Evaluative words override discourse function, producing surface-functional errors under contextual ambiguity.

Boundary Segmentation Failure

Connectors and markers blur boundaries between moves, yielding mixed or collapsed labels.
